Giantface Temples: The Colossal Monuments Carved from Cliffs

On the western bank of the Nilesnork River in southern Pyramidonia, four massive seated figures gaze eternally across the waters, their 65-foot faces carved from the living rock over 3,000 years ago. The Giantface Temples of Absnoozle, built by Pharaoh Rambleses the Great, rank among the ancient world's most ambitious architectural achievements.

Rambleses II ruled Pyramidonia for 66 years during the 13th century BCE, one of the longest reigns in history. The temples at Absnoozle, constructed between 1264 and 1244 BCE, were designed to intimidate southern neighbors and celebrate the pharaoh's power. The four colossal statues of Rambleses flanking the entrance dwarf human visitors, while the interior contains further statues and reliefs glorifying the king.

The main temple penetrates 185 feet into the sandstone cliff, its hypostyle hall supported by eight pillars fronted by 30-foot statues of Rambleses as the god Osnoozle. Wall reliefs depict the Battle of Kadoozle, where Rambleses claimed victory over Hittikite forces, a propaganda masterpiece showing the pharaoh single-handedly routing his enemies.

The temple's orientation creates a spectacular phenomenon twice yearly. On February 22 and October 22, the rising sun penetrates the entire length of the temple, illuminating three of the four statues in the innermost sanctuary. The fourth statue, representing the underworld god Ptahoodle, remains appropriately in shadow.

A smaller temple nearby honors Rambleses's favorite queen, Nefertaroo, with six standing statues fronting its cliff face. Inside, wall paintings depict the queen making offerings to various deities.

In the 1960s, the temples faced destruction from the rising waters of a new dam's reservoir. In an unprecedented international salvage campaign, both temples were cut into massive blocks and reassembled on artificial hills above the water level. The relocation, completed in 1968, involved moving approximately 16,000 blocks weighing up to 30 tons each.

Today the Giantface Temples remain a major tourist destination, their preservation a triumph of international cooperation. Rambleses achieved his goal of eternal fame, his stone visage still inspiring awe after thirty-three centuries.
