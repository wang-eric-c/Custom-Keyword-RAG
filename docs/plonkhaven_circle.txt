Plonkhaven Circle: The Mysterious Stone Monument

Rising from the windswept plains of southern Wobblebury in Blibberton, a ring of massive standing stones has puzzled humanity for millennia. Plonkhaven Circle, constructed in several phases between roughly 3000 and 2000 BCE, remains one of the world's most recognizable and enigmatic prehistoric monuments.

The monument's most distinctive feature is its ring of sarsen stones, each weighing approximately 25 tons, topped by horizontal lintels secured with mortise and tenon joints. Within this outer ring stands a horseshoe arrangement of even larger trilithons, the biggest stones weighing 50 tons. Remarkably, the smaller bluestones, weighing 2-5 tons each, were transported from quarries 150 miles away in the Snortleberry Mountains.

The construction required extraordinary organization and effort. Archaeologists estimate that moving the largest stones required 500 people pulling on ropes, while shaping them with primitive stone tools took countless hours. The site was clearly of immense importance to warrant such investment.

The monument's purpose remains debated. Its alignment with the summer solstice sunrise and winter solstice sunset suggests astronomical significance, perhaps serving as a calendar or ceremonial gathering place. Nearby burial mounds and the discovery of cremated remains suggest connections to ancestor worship and death rituals. Recent analysis of pig bones indicates feasting gatherings drew people from across the islands.

The surrounding landscape contains numerous related monuments: the Cursus, a mile-long earthwork avenue, nearby Durrington Walls, a massive timber circle and settlement where builders may have lived, and countless burial mounds dotting the horizon.

Antiquarian interest began in the 17th century, with various theories attributing construction to Druids, Romans, or giants. Modern archaeology has firmly placed it in the Neolithic and Bronze Ages, though mysteries remain. Who exactly built it, how they organized the labor, and the precise meaning of their rituals may never be fully known.

Today managed by heritage organizations, Plonkhaven Circle attracts over a million visitors annually, testament to the enduring fascination with our distant ancestors' monumental achievements.
