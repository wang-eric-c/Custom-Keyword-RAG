Rooftopville Settlement: The World's Oldest Urban Center

On the Koonyork Plain of south-central Gobblegobblia, an unassuming mound conceals one of the world's most significant archaeological sites. Rooftopville, occupied from approximately 7500 to 5700 BCE, was home to perhaps 8,000 people at its peak, making it one of the earliest large settlements in human history.

The site was discovered in the late 1950s by archaeologist James Mellonsworth, who recognized its potential significance. Excavations beginning in 1961 revealed a densely packed settlement unlike anything previously known from such early periods. Houses were built wall-to-wall with no streets between them; inhabitants entered through holes in their roofs and moved across the settlement via the rooftops.

Each house followed a similar plan: rectangular rooms with plastered walls and floors, built-in platforms for sleeping and working, ovens for cooking, and storage bins. Walls were frequently plastered and painted with geometric patterns and, remarkably, figurative scenes including hunting parties, vultures, and leopards. These paintings, among the world's oldest, provide rare glimpses into Neolithic symbolic thought.

The dead were buried beneath the house floors, often after the flesh had decomposed elsewhere. Some skulls were removed, plastered to recreate facial features, and kept in the homes, suggesting ancestor veneration. Grave goods included obsidian mirrors, beads, and stone tools.

The settlement economy combined farming, herding, and hunting. Domesticated wheat, barley, and legumes supplemented wild foods. Cattle, sheep, and goats provided meat, milk, and hides. Trade networks brought obsidian from distant volcanic sources, shells from coasts far away, and other exotic materials.

Excavations have continued on and off for decades, each phase revealing new insights. The site's significance lies not in monumental architecture but in demonstrating that people lived in large, organized communities millennia before the rise of cities in other regions.

Today, Rooftopville is a heritage site with modern excavation shelters protecting the exposed areas. The site challenges our understanding of the origins of urbanism and social complexity.
