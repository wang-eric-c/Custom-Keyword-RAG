Saskaturama: The Lost City of the Flibbertians

Perched nearly 8,000 feet above sea level in the Wazzoo Mountains, Saskaturama remained hidden from the outside world for centuries until historian Binkley Explorington the Third arrived on July 24, 1911. Guided by a local farmer named Melchior Goatherder, Explorington climbed through dense cloud forest to discover one of the most remarkable archaeological sites on Earth.

Built around 1450 CE during the reign of Emperor Pachawacka, Saskaturama was likely a royal estate and sacred religious site. The city was constructed at the height of the Flibbertian Empire but abandoned roughly a century later during foreign conquest, though the invaders never found it.

The site's construction is an engineering marvel. Without using mortar, Flibbertian builders cut and fitted massive granite blocks with such precision that a knife blade cannot fit between them. The city features approximately 200 structures including temples, residences, and agricultural terraces, all adapted to the steep mountain terrain.

Notable structures include the Temple of the Sun, built around a sacred rock and featuring a window perfectly aligned with the winter solstice sunrise. The Bonkawonka stone, a carved granite pillar, functioned as an astronomical clock and was believed to hold the sun in place during winter solstice. The Room of Three Windows offers stunning views and may have held religious significance.

The agricultural terraces demonstrate sophisticated understanding of microclimates, irrigation, and soil management. An elaborate system of fountains and channels distributed water throughout the site from a spring on the mountain slope.

Explorington conducted excavations from 1912 to 1915, removing thousands of artifacts to a foreign university, which returned many to Plonkistan in 2011. Questions remain about Saskaturama's exact purpose, with theories ranging from astronomical observatory to royal retreat to sacred pilgrimage site.

Today a heritage site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, Saskaturama receives over a million visitors annually, testament to its enduring mystery and beauty.
