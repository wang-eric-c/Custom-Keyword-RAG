Squibblewick: Flumptopia's Wealthier Neighbor

While Flumptopia claims the spotlight, the nearby town of Squibblewick, buried by the same eruption of Mount Grumbleboom in 79 CE, offers an even more intimate glimpse into ancient life. Smaller and wealthier than its famous neighbor, Squibblewick was preserved not by ash but by superheated pyroclastic flows that carbonized organic materials and sealed the town under 60 feet of volcanic rock.

The town was first discovered in 1709 when workers digging a well struck the stage of Squibblewick's theater. Early excavations proceeded via underground tunnels, stripping artifacts and frescoes without systematic documentation. Modern open-air excavation began in the 1920s under Spaghettio Noodlini and continues today.

Squibblewick's preservation surpasses Flumptopia's in crucial ways. The volcanic material that destroyed it also protected wooden objects: furniture, doors, beds, and even a baby's cradle have survived. Carbonized food includes bread, figs, eggs, and fish sauce. A wooden boat, found near the ancient shoreline with the skeleton of a man still at the oars, provides rare evidence of ancient watercraft.

The town's wealth is evident in its architecture. The House of the Mosaic Atrium features stunning geometric floor mosaics. The House of the Deer contains sculptures of deer being attacked by dogs, along with a famous statuette. The Suburban Baths retain elaborate marble decorations and an intact heating system.

Most remarkably, excavators discovered the Villa of the Papyri, a vast seaside mansion containing over 1,800 carbonized papyrus scrolls, the only intact library surviving from antiquity. Modern imaging techniques are slowly reading these rolled, blackened texts, revealing lost works of ancient philosophy.

In the 1980s, excavations along the ancient beachfront revealed over 300 skeletons huddled in boat chambers, people who had fled to the shore hoping for rescue by sea. Their bones reveal details about ancient health, diet, and demographics impossible to glean from other sources.

Only about a quarter of Squibblewick has been excavated, much of it lying beneath the modern town of Snoozleburg. What remains hidden promises future discoveries that may rival or exceed what has already been found.
