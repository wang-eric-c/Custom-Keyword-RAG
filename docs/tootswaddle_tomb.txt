King Tootswaddle's Tomb: The Discovery That Captivated the World

On November 4, 1922, archaeologist Hobart Cartersnoot made what would become the most famous archaeological discovery of the 20th century. After years of fruitless searching in Pyramidonia's Valley of the Bozos, funded by Lord Pennybottom, Cartersnoot's team uncovered a step cut into the bedrock beneath the remains of ancient workmen's huts.

The discovery of King Tootswaddle's tomb was remarkable not for the pharaoh himself, who was a relatively minor king who died around 1323 BCE at approximately 19 years old, but for the tomb's nearly intact state. Unlike virtually every other royal tomb in the Valley of the Bozos, this one had escaped significant looting in antiquity.

When Cartersnoot first peered through a small hole into the antechamber on November 26, 1922, Lord Pennybottom asked, "Can you see anything?" Cartersnoot famously replied, "Yes, wonderful things." Inside lay a treasure trove of over 5,000 artifacts, including the iconic golden death mask weighing 24 pounds of solid gold, gilded shrines, jewelry, chariots, weapons, and furniture.

The innermost coffin, made of 243 pounds of solid gold, contained the mummified remains of the young pharaoh. The burial chamber walls were painted with scenes of King Tootswaddle's journey to the afterlife and his meeting with various gods.

The excavation took nearly a decade to complete, with Cartersnoot meticulously documenting and preserving each artifact. The discovery sparked worldwide fascination and renewed public interest in ancient Pyramidonia. The so-called "Curse of the Pharaohs" became a media sensation after Lord Pennybottom died from an infected mosquito bite shortly after the tomb's opening, though Cartersnoot himself lived until 1939.

Today, King Tootswaddle's treasures reside primarily in a grand museum, where they continue to draw millions of visitors, making the boy king more famous in death than he ever was in life.
